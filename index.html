Microscopy

To understand the working principle of a Microscope and its parts.
Principle

Cells smaller than 0.1 mm cannot be seen by the naked eye. A compound microscope uses two lens systems — the ocular (eyepiece) and the objective lens — together to magnify the specimen.

Total Magnification = Ocular Lens Magnification × Objective Lens Magnification

Key Parts
PartFunction
Ocular/EyepiecePrimary view point; magnification inscribed on it (usually 10×)
Objective LensFour lenses on rotating turret: 4×, 10×, 40×, 100×
Coarse AdjustmentLarge knob; use only at 4× and 10×
Fine AdjustmentSmall knob; use at 40× and 100×
Condenser LensFocuses light on specimen; below the stage
Iris DiaphragmRegulates light intensity passing through
StageHorizontal platform holding the slide
Nose PieceRotating turret holding objective lenses
Resolution & Numerical Aperture

Resolving Distance = smallest distance between two points that can be seen as separate.

Formula R.D. = wavelength / (2 × N.A.)

Numerical Aperture (N.A.) = N × sin α
N = refractive index of medium between object and lens  |  α = half the angular aperture
  • Blue light (360–420 nm) gives better resolution than red light (650–800 nm)
  • Oil immersion (N = 1.52) improves N.A. over air (N = 1.00)
  • Example: With oil lens N.A. = 1.25 and blue light (400 nm): R.D. = 400/(2×1.25) = 160 nm = 0.16 µm
Procedure (Observation of Prepared Slides)
  1. Clean ocular and objective lenses; set coarse knob to maximum working distance.
  2. Place slide on stage and secure in slide holder.
  3. Rotate objective to 4×; focus with coarse adjustment knob.
  4. Switch to 10×; refocus.
  5. Switch to 40×; use only fine adjustment. Adjust iris diaphragm for light.
  6. Rotate nosepiece halfway; add a drop of immersion oil on the slide.
  7. Rotate to 100× (lens should dip into oil); focus with fine adjustment only.
  8. After use: bring to max working distance, clean all lenses, return microscope.
Result
Expected outcome Slides were observed and total magnification calculated = ______× (e.g. 10× ocular × 40× objective = 400×)

Cell Counting Using Hemocytometer

To count the number of cells in a bacterial/blood suspension using a hemocytometer.
Principle

The hemocytometer (invented by Louis-Charles Malassez) is a special microscope slide with two chambers, each divided into 9 large squares (1 mm × 1 mm). Cover slip is held 0.1 mm above the chamber, creating a defined volume for counting.

DimensionsAreaColourVolume (0.1 mm depth)
1 × 1 mm1 mm²Red100 nl
0.25 × 0.25 mm (1/16)0.0625 mm²Green6.25 nl
0.20 × 0.20 mm (1/25)0.04 mm²Yellow4 nl
0.05 × 0.05 mm (1/400)0.0025 mm²Blue0.25 nl
Materials

Hemocytometer, coverslips, microscope glass slides, bacterial/blood culture, micropipettes, microscope.

Procedure
  1. Clean hemocytometer and coverslip with 70% ethanol; place coverslip on chamber.
  2. Take 10 µL of sample and load into chamber using micropipette (capillary action fills the chamber).
  3. Gently mix suspension; fill second chamber similarly. Remove excess liquid with tissue.
  4. Place on microscope stage; observe under 10× (low power) to check uniform distribution.
  5. Switch to 40×; focus on central RBC squares.
  6. Count cells in 5 medium-sized corner RBC squares (4 corners + 1 central = 5 squares, total 80 small squares).
Calculations
RBC Count Formula Total RBCs/mm³ = N × Dilution Factor / (Area × Depth)

Where:
  Area of 5 squares = 5/25 = 1/5 mm²
  Depth = 1/10 mm = 0.1 mm
  Dilution Factor = 200

= N × 200 / (1/5 × 0.1) = N × 200 × 50 = N × 10,000

Example: If N = 500 → Total RBCs = 500 × 10,000 = 5,000,000 (5.0 million/mm³)
Result
Expected outcome Number of cells present in the blood sample = ___________ cells/mm³

Blood Smear Preparation and Observation

To identify the different types of human blood cells in a blood smear.
Principle

Blood = 44% blood cells + 56% plasma. Three types of blood cells:

  • RBCs (Erythrocytes) — disk-shaped, enucleated, ~7.2 µm diameter
  • WBCs (Leukocytes) — nucleated; includes granulocytes, lymphocytes, monocytes
  • Platelets (Thrombocytes) — for clotting
Cell TypeCytoplasm StainNucleus Stain
RBCsDark pink to redNil (no nucleus)
NeutrophilsPurple / dark pinkDeep blue-violet (multilobed)
EosinophilsOrange to pink—
BasophilsDeep blue to violet—
LymphocytesLight blueDeep blue-violet (large, spherical)
MonocytesPale grey-blueDeep bluish-purple
Materials

Glass slides, sterilised needles, human blood, spirit lamp, 70% ethanol, distilled water, Giemsa stain, methanol, Pasteur pipettes, cotton, tissue paper, microscope.

Procedure — A: Smear Preparation
  1. Wipe two clean glass slides with 70% ethanol.
  2. Clean finger with alcohol; prick with sterilised needle.
  3. Place a small drop of blood at one end of slide.
  4. Hold second slide at 30° angle; touch blood drop and spread in one smooth motion.
  5. A good smear shows a feathered edge and separated RBCs.
  6. Allow to air dry completely.
Procedure — B: Giemsa Staining
  1. Fix dried smear by immersing in methanol for 5–10 seconds.
  2. Apply Giemsa staining solution for 10 seconds.
  3. Wash with distilled water to remove excess stain.
  4. Air dry for 5 minutes.
  5. Observe under microscope at high power; identify all cell types.
Result
Expected outcome Different blood cells identified: RBCs (pink, no nucleus), Neutrophils (multilobed nucleus), Basophils (dark blue granules), Eosinophils (orange granules), Lymphocytes (large spherical nucleus), Monocytes (kidney-shaped nucleus).

Mitosis in Onion Root Tip

To observe mitotic cell division stages in onion root tip cells.
Principle

Mitosis occurs in meristematic cells (undifferentiated, rapidly dividing). Root tips are ideal because cell division is rapid and clearly visible. Strong acid (HCl) breaks down cellulosic bonds in plant cell walls, allowing Feulgen stain to penetrate and stain chromosomes.

Materials

Freshly sprouted onion root tips, Feulgen stain, 45% acetic acid, 1N HCl, Carnoy's fixative, surgical blades, water bath (60°C), 70% ethanol, microcentrifuge tubes, micropipettes, coverslips, microscope.

Procedure
  1. Cut ~6 mm root tip from sprouting onion.
  2. Place 5–6 root tips in microcentrifuge tube.
  3. Add 1 mL Carnoy's fixative; incubate for 24 hours.
  4. Transfer root tips to fresh tube; add 1 mL of 1N HCl.
  5. Incubate at 60°C for 15 minutes in water bath (breaks cell wall).
  6. Remove HCl; add 0.5 mL Feulgen stain; incubate 10–15 minutes.
  7. Place one drop of 45% acetic acid on a clean slide.
  8. Place red-stained root tip on slide using scalpel.
  9. Cover with coverslip; gently tap to form a monolayer of cells.
  10. Observe under microscope; identify mitotic stages.
Mitotic Stages — What to Observe
StageKey Visual Features
InterphaseCondensed chromosome; nucleus visible; cell preparing to divide
ProphaseThread-like chromosome structures becoming visible
MetaphaseSpindle fibres visible; distinct chromosomes aligned at cell centre (metaphase plate)
AnaphaseSister chromatids pulled towards opposite poles
TelophaseCell undergoes equal division; two nuclei forming
Result
Expected outcome Different stages of mitotic cell division — Interphase, Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase — are clearly observed in the onion root tip squash preparation.

Cell Viability — Trypan Blue Exclusion

To determine cell viability using membrane-permeable dye (Trypan Blue).
Principle

Trypan Blue Dye Exclusion Test is based on the fact that live cells have intact membranes that exclude dyes like trypan blue, while dead cells cannot exclude the dye.

  • Viable cell → clear/unstained cytoplasm
  • Non-viable (dead) cell → blue-filled cytoplasm
  • Viability ≥ 95% = excellent
Safety Trypan blue is a potential carcinogen (MSDS). Always wear gloves and lab coat. Count within 5 minutes — cell death increases with longer exposure.
Materials

Pipette and tips, 0.4% Trypan Blue solution, hemocytometer, coverslip, microscope, counter, 1.5 mL centrifuge tubes, cell suspension.

Procedure
  1. Place 50 µL cell suspension in 1.5 mL centrifuge tube.
  2. Add 50 µL of 0.4% Trypan Blue (1:2 dilution); mix by pipetting up and down.
  3. Incubate for < 3 minutes at room temperature (do not exceed 5 minutes).
  4. Load 10–20 µL into one side of hemocytometer (coverslip already in place).
  5. Place on microscope stage; focus on cells.
  6. Count all cells (clear + blue) in each large corner square (each has 16 small squares). Note blue cells separately.
  7. Calculate % viability.
Calculation
Formula % Viable Cells = [1 – (No. of blue cells ÷ Total cells)] × 100

Or: % Viable = (Viable cells / Total cells) × 100
Note Alert supervisor if viability < 90%.
Result
Expected outcome Dark blue-filled cells = non-viable (dead). Cells with only a blue outline / clear interior = viable. % Viable cells = ___________

Fluorescent Labelling (AO/EB Staining)

To label and observe cellular components using fluorescent dyes (Acridine Orange + Ethidium Bromide).
Principle

Acridine Orange (AO) is a cell-permeable nucleic acid selective fluorescent dye — stains both live and dead cells. Ethidium Bromide (EB) can only enter cells that have lost membrane integrity.

Cell StatusAO AloneAO + EB Combined
Live/ViableUniformly greenGreen
ApoptoticGreen + bright green dots (condensed chromatin)Green with nuclear fragmentation
NecroticOrange (weak/disappearing fluorescence)Orange; nuclear morphology resembles viable cells — no condensed chromatin
Safety AO and EB are potential carcinogens (MSDS). Always wear gloves and lab coat.
Materials

Pipette and tips, Acridine Orange (5 mg/mL in ethanol), Ethidium Bromide (3 mg/mL in absolute ethanol), PBS, glass slides, coverslips, fluorescent microscope, cultured cells.

Procedure
  1. Seed cultured cells onto coverslip at 2×10⁴ cells/mL; allow to grow.
  2. Remove media; wash with PBS.
  3. Prepare working dye solution:
    • Take 1 µL of 3 mg/mL EB stock + 1 µL of 5 mg/mL AO stock
    • Add PBS to make final volume 1 mL; mix well
  4. Add AO/EB solution to cells; incubate.
  5. Remove dye solution; wash cells with PBS.
  6. Mount coverslip on glass slide.
  7. Observe under fluorescent microscope.
Result
Expected outcome Green fluorescence = viable cells with active nucleus. Red/orange fluorescence = fragmented nucleus (apoptosis / necrosis). Bright green nuclear dots = chromatin condensation (early apoptosis).
